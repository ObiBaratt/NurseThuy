About Me

This is what I do.

My name is Thuy Tran, a recently naturalized U.S. citizen from Vietnam. Since I was young, I have idolized the nurses and doctors who saved my father from a life-threatening liver condition and have been passionate about pursuing a career in health care. I came here with almost nothing and step by step have overcome the obstacles present in an alien culture and educational system to pursue my dream of helping others as a nurse. I began by volunteering at the Kaiser South ER where I was able to help patients and their families during their times of hardship and admired the staff that efficiently reacted to emergencies. At Sacramento State, I participated in clinical rotations and a preceptorship at many of the local hospitals, where my inquisitiveness and decisiveness were noted both by my clinical faculties, the nurses I was assigned to work under, as well as supervising physicians.

In my final clinical evaluation, it was noted that I “used all resources available to seek out answers, often even approaching providers to ask questions when nurses couldn’t resolve my inquiries.” (Tenaya Natov, RN, Clinical Instructor). I always wanted to make sure I knew as much as possible about my assigned patients in order to secure the best possible outcomes for them. This paid off when I was instrumental in saving a newborn’s life during my Obstetrics rotation at Mercy San Juan. I was also commended in my ER clinical rotation at Mercy General by the staff and physicians for my decisive actions in helping a patient to the catheterization lab after a STEMI. In my work at UCDMC as an Extern on D14, I also alerted staff to a patient with an obstructed airway during the night shift and worked with the charge nurse and the patient’s primary nurse to help clear his airway and restore normal breathing.

All my hard work and determination was rewarded with the opportunity to have my preceptorship in the ER. I was progressively more responsible for patient care, under my preceptor’s supervision, and got a taste of what it is like to work as an RN in the ER. I experienced the upsides and the downsides of working with critical patients both in my preceptorship and when an unborn child died right before a scheduled C-section. I knew I had to continue nursing to be there to help those that I could and do my best for the families of those that I could not.

It has taken resilience, plenty of hard work and diligence to get where I am now, and I am so thankful for those that supported me along the way. I want to be a part of a team that creates a culture of inclusion, support, and professionalism for the ultimate goal of reliable and exceptional teamwork and patient care.

Sincerely, Thuy Tran
